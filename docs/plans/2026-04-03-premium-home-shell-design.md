# Premium Home Shell Design

## Goal

Redesign only the homepage and global shell so Rolecraft reads as a premium SaaS workspace rather than an editorial front page. The new direction should feel modern, productized, and trustworthy while preserving the product's human-in-the-loop stance.

## Product Fit

Rolecraft is not a growth dashboard and not a consumer app. It helps a serious job seeker move through a structured workflow where automation assists but never silently replaces judgment. The homepage and shell should therefore communicate competence, control, and clarity before they communicate novelty.

## Chosen Direction: Executive Workspace

This direction blends premium SaaS polish with a calmer operational feel.

- The shell should feel like the frame of a high-end product, with a strong brand block, clearer navigation, and a compact utility row.
- The homepage should feel like a product landing surface inside the app, not a magazine layout and not a generic marketing page.
- The visual system should be cleaner and cooler than the prior crafted/editorial passes.

## Visual Language

### Tone

Confident, precise, modern, and premium. The interface should feel intentionally designed for decision-making work.

### Color

Use slate and cloud neutrals with restrained amber and mint accents. Avoid purple, neon, and loud gradient-heavy startup styling. Dark mode should feel like a refined control surface, not a glowing cyber dashboard.

### Typography

Use a sharper product hierarchy with bold sans-driven headings and supporting body copy. Keep display flourishes minimal. The typography should read like a polished SaaS app rather than a publication.

### Surfaces

Lean into polished panels, elevated rails, and grouped product sections. Cards are acceptable here, but they should feel structural and premium rather than repetitive or decorative.

## Shell Changes

- Add a stronger utility row that frames the workspace and theme control.
- Rebuild the brand block so it feels like a premium product header rather than a masthead.
- Make navigation feel more like an app control bar with clearer active states.
- Keep the status pill, but recast it as a product trust signal rather than editorial copy.

## Homepage Changes

- Replace the editorial front-page layout with a premium hero split.
- Put the core product promise, short support copy, and primary actions on the left.
- Use the right side for a compact workflow proof rail that shows the product's operating model.
- Add a strong proof band below the hero for the key product traits: local-first context, reviewable automation, manual submission.
- Add a lower workflow section that explains the three-stage loop in a more polished product language.
- Keep direct entry points for Profile, Settings, and Jobs, but make them feel like workspace launch pads rather than article links.

## Guardrails

- Do not drift back into editorial or magazine tropes.
- Do not use purple neon or flashy glows.
- Do not over-market the product. The copy should still sound grounded and operational.
- Do not redesign other pages in this pass.

## Success Criteria

- The homepage immediately reads as a premium SaaS product surface.
- The shell feels materially different from the editorial pass.
- The user can quickly judge whether this direction is closer to the intended taste before deeper page redesign work begins.
